Leon’s Proposed Trail
1. Tour Overview
Tour Theme: The LA Nobody Puts On a Poster
Target Audience: Gen Z creatives, culture-seekers, and ﬁrst-time LA visitors who came to ﬁnd the city, not the postcard.
Total Tour Duration: 8 hours (full day)
Tour Price Per Person: $35
What Is NOT Included:
* Food and drinks: ~$25–45 depending on appetite
* Transportation (Uber between stops): ~$20–30
* Entry fees where applicable: ~$0–10
—
Tour Summary Questions
Why would Gen Z speciﬁcally enjoy this experience?
Because Gen Z has an extraordinary radar for the performative, and Los Angeles — if you let it — will perform at you all day long.
This tour is the antidote. Every stop on this route is a place where actual Angelenos go when they’re not performing anything for
anyone. The food is real, the art is unsanitized, the views cost nothing, and none of it will look like something you’ve already scrolled
past. It will look like something you found. That distinction matters more to this generation than any other, because they grew up
knowing the difference.
Why did you pick this tour route? What do you personally like to do at these spots?
I attended Claremont McKenna College from 1999 to 2002, which put me close enough to Los Angeles to spend real time there —
not tourist time, but the slow, directionless, figure-it-out-yourself time that actually teaches you a city. I wasn’t staying in hotels or
following recommendations. I was driving the wrong direction and finding things. The stops on this route are the descendants of that

kind of discovery. They are places I would take someone I actually cared about impressing, which means no safety nets and no
compromises.
After completing the tour, what would you like customers to feel, and what specific words and comments would you hope to hear in
their feedback?
I want them to feel like they were let in on something. Not like they were toured — like they were trusted. The words I’d want to hear
are: “I didn’t know this existed.” “This felt real.” “I’m coming back.” And the one I’d want most: “I already sent this to five people.”

2. Transportation Summary
Primary Transportation Method: Uber between major stops, walking within neighborhoods
Estimated Travel Time Between Stops:
* Stop 1 → Stop 2: 12 minutes
* Stop 2 → Stop 3: 18 minutes
* Stop 3 → Stop 4: 10 minutes
* Stop 4 → Stop 5: 15 minutes
* Stop 5 → Stop 6: 20 minutes
Brief Explanation of Transportation Choice:
LA is not a walking city the way New York is, but it rewards walking ferociously once you’re inside a neighborhood. The strategy here
is Uber between nodes, then surrender to the block. All stops are in a pocket of the city dense enough to explore on foot, which is
where the real discoveries live — the side street, the unmarked door, the mural that has no plaque. The transit is the punctuation.
The walking is the sentence.

3. Tour Stops
---
Stop 1
* Location Name: Guisados
* Neighborhood: Boyle Heights
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/z2vNa7H9WSE19xPb6?g_st=ic

* Overview of the Spot: A small, unassuming taco counter on East Cesar Chavez that has been quietly serving some of the
most precise, thoughtful Mexican food in the city for over a decade. The braised meat tacos are made from scratch daily and the
lines move fast. The room is small, the tables are communal, and the energy is entirely neighborhood.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: Because every great day in Los Angeles should begin somewhere that reminds you the city
existed long before the industry arrived. Boyle Heights is one of the oldest continuously inhabited neighborhoods in LA, and
Guisados sits in it like it belongs there — because it does. This is not a restaurant that discovered its neighborhood. It grew out of it.
Gen Z travelers who want to understand Los Angeles have to start east of the 110, and they have to start hungry.
---
Stop 2
* Location Name: Self Help Graphics &amp; Art
* Neighborhood: Boyle Heights
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/AFsRevpAoPJYamJp8?g_st=ic
* Overview of the Spot: A nonprofit art space and printmaking studio founded in 1970 that has been at the center of Chicano art
and culture in Los Angeles for more than fifty years. The gallery rotates exhibitions by local and regional artists, and the building itself
— murals floor to ceiling, courtyard open to the street — is one of the most visually arresting spaces in the city.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: Self Help Graphics is the kind of institution that defines what community-driven art actually
means, not as a branding concept but as a fifty-year practice. For a generation that can identify performative allyship from three
blocks away, this place is the real thing. The work on the walls is political, personal, and technically accomplished, often
simultaneously. You will not see anything like it at a gallery in Silver Lake, and you will feel the difference the moment you walk in.
—
Stop 3
* Location Name: The LA River Bike Path (Elysian Valley / Frogtown stretch)
* Neighborhood: Elysian Valley (Frogtown)
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/C9rLbaYTBiKWt7rF9?g_st=ic
* Overview of the Spot: A two-mile stretch of the LA River running through Frogtown where the concrete channel gives way to a
soft-bottom section with actual water, herons, and a bike and walking path that feels categorically different from the rest of the city.

Studios, small galleries, and cafés have opened along the riverbank in the last decade, creating one of the most surprising and
genuinely delightful urban walks in Los Angeles.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: Most visitors don’t know the LA River exists in any form worth visiting, because most of it
doesn’t. This stretch is the exception, and the contrast —you are standing next to water, in Los Angeles, surrounded by street art and
birds — lands differently in person than any photograph can prepare you for. It is also intensely photogenic in a style that feels
accidental rather than curated, which is exactly the register Gen Z trusts.
—
Stop 4
* Location Name: Cognoscenti Coffee / Eightfold Coffee
* Neighborhood: Glassell Park
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/L2s3rb8hVv44RnLN7?g_st=ic
* Overview of the Spot: A serious, unpretentious specialty coffee operation tucked into a residential stretch of Glassell Park, a
neighborhood that most Angelenos couldn’t locate on a map ten years ago and that now houses some of the city’s most interesting
small businesses. The coffee is exceptional, the space is calm, and the clientele is almost entirely local.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: The third wave coffee movement in Los Angeles has produced a lot of beautiful rooms full
of people performing the act of drinking coffee. Cognoscenti is not that. It is a place that takes the product seriously and takes the
neighborhood seriously, which in LA is a rarer combination than it should be. This is a genuine rest stop — a place to sit, regroup,
and have the kind of conversation the morning’s experiences were worth having.
—
Stop 5
* Location Name: Donut Friend
* Neighborhood: Highland Park
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/PCCtUGqHaoYqRzmq5?g_st=ic
* Overview of the Spot: A punk-rock-themed, fully vegan donut shop on York Boulevard in which every donut is named after a
band and the menu reads like a playlist from a show you wish you’d been at. The donuts are extraordinary — the kind that make you

reconsider whether you’d been eating donuts correctly before — and the shop is exactly as devoted to its aesthetic as it sounds,
without a single note of irony.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: Donut Friend is the clearest illustration of what Highland Park actually is: a neighborhood
where counterculture took root and refused to perform its own death for the benefit of development. Gen Z will understand this place
immediately and completely. It is also, independent of everything else, one of the best things you can eat in Los Angeles, which
should never be beside the point.
—
Stop 6
* Location Name: The Oaks Gourmet Market
* Neighborhood: Franklin Village / Los Feliz
* Google Maps URL: https://maps.app.goo.gl/SeexSF4nAdANKi356?g_st=ic
* Overview of the Spot: A neighborhood market and café tucked into the small, walkable commercial strip of Franklin Village
that operates simultaneously as a serious bottle shop, a deli, and one of the best casual dining spots in the city. The back patio is
shaded, the charcuterie is excellent, and the entire operation seems as if it was designed for people who live nearby and want
something genuinely good without a reservation or a valet.
* Why This Spot Is Recommended: Franklin Village is one of the last truly walkable neighborhood strips in Los Angeles —
small enough to cover on foot, specific enough to feel like a discovery, and populated almost entirely by people who actually live
there. The Oaks is its anchor, and ending the day here, on the back patio with a glass of something worth drinking, is the appropriate
conclusion to a day spent finding the city nobody puts on a poster. You came looking for the real Los Angeles. This is what it feels
like to have found it.
